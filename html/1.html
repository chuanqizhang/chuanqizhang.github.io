Abstract
The behaviors of customers vary when they shop online
and some of the information could indicate their purchase
abilities. So we access the dataset about Customer Pur-
chase Predictions from kaggle.com. There are two moti-
vations set up for this project. One is to apply the model
named Gradient Boosting Decision Tree (abbreviated as
GBDT) on the dataset to produce the best estimation. The
other is to compare the performance between GBDT and
Random Forest, especially in the bias and variance decom-
position dimension. As the crucial step of the whole task,
data processing is conducted to deal with the raw dataset,
including the transformation of JSON format, the dummy-
lization of categorical variables and also the consideration
of the collinearity. Then, for both goals, we perform ac-
complished R packages on the processed data to do the pre-
diction and calculate the performance coefficients, namely
Mean Squared Error for the evaluation. Specifically for
the comparison part, we execute bias-variance decomposi-
tion and validate the theoretical conclusion we deduce pre-
viously. Using all the results and conclusions, we can il-
lustrate, to some extent, the characteristic of the two mod-
els and the No Free Lunch theorem in the macro Machine
Learning dimension.
1. Introduction
The original aim of our project is to construct a semi-
supervised algorithm to build up a connection between
the basic information of a customer and his/her purchase
amount at store, which in common sense have some rela-
tionship in between and provides an important guidance to
management. After a period of study, we find the tree-based
models are pretty appropriate to achieve our goal. So we try
to apply the method - Gradient Boosting Decision Tree and
compare it with Random Forest, which is the core of the
project.
We have a dataset collected by Google Merchandise
Store on Kaggle’s website. It offers several features of cus-
tomers and the transaction revenue of the purchase; the latter
is the response variable of the model. Each piece of infor-
mation has a unique ID attached to identify them. But there
are some data missing in the dataset even in the revenue col-
umn. Our goal is to connect the features with the transaction
revenue and predict the revenue for newly arrived customers
with different regression models, then compare them by the
MSE caculation and the bias-variance decomposition
1.1. Data Processing
The basic data-cleaning includes the filtration, erasure
and interpolation of data. For our project, the initial task
is to normalize the different types of data so as to do the fur-
ther processing. After investigating into the dataset, we ap-
ply dummy variables to obtain the categorical feature, which
helps the models identity their information in a correct way
and apply to the regression. Subsequently, we work on find-
ing all the unavailable data and delete them with more NA
values than a threshold set up. Finally, we do the interpo-
lation with mode values so that the complete dataset comes
up.
1.2. Models
For the complete dataset, the best-fit approaches we
choose are Gradient Boosting Decision Tree and Random
Forest.
GBDT is an ensemble method for fitting statistical mod-
els that differs fundamentally from conventional techniques
that aim to fit a single parsimonious model. Boosted regres-
1
sion trees combine the strengths of two algorithms: regres-
sion trees (models that relate a response to their predictors
by recursive binary splits) and boosting (an adaptive method
for combining many simple models to give improved pre-
dictive performance). The final GBDT model can be under-
stood as an additive regression model.[1]
Random forests are an ensemble learning method for
classification and regression that operate by constructing a
multitude of decision trees at training time and outputting
the class that is the mode of the classes output by individual
trees. The method combines “bagging” idea and the ran-
dom selection of features in order to construct a collection
of decision trees with controlled variance.[2]
The algorithm in details will be demonstrated in the third
section - Proposed Method.
1.3. Evaluation
As we are aiming at continuous regression instead of dis-
crete classification, MSE could be a better metric to evaluate
rather than accuracy analysis. Furthermore, bias-variance
decomposition will more specifically indicate the factor of
whether the model is success or not. From we have dis-
cussed in class, a high bias can show the model’s disabil-
ity to learn relations between features effectively and hence
would underfit on the dataset leading to low accuracy while
predicting. By contrast, a high variance indicates the pre-
dicted output become highly flexible with respect to the data
points of the dataset, which causes a model to overfit on the
training data leading to low accuracy while predicting. So
they are the key points considered for measuring success in
our project, and we calculate according to the formulas as

of Statistical Learning [3] mainly talked about the Gradi-
ent Boosting theory. It interprets the procedure of Gradi-
ent Boosting in Tree Algorithm, which uses base regression

• Then for those missing categorical data, we im-

3.3. Hyperparameters of GBDT
In this algorithm (especially in this R function of the
package “gbm”), we have 3 hyperparameters to tune.
Shrinkage: a factor between 0 and 1. It will shrink the
significance of the next tree in order to avoid overfitting.
n.trees and interaction.depth: parameters related to
pruning and at the same time, we have cross-validation to
validate the model performance.
